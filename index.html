What this is. A flow picture of national public finances — a different thing from the Tax Atlas, which catalogues the mechanisms of taxation without amounts. This chart needs magnitudes, so it uses rounded 2025-26 forecast figures from the Office for Budget Responsibility's March 2026 public-finance forecast, supplemented by ONS and House of Commons Library material where noted.

Notes. Figures are approximate and rounded, and totals balance by construction. "Other taxes" bundles capital gains tax, inheritance tax, stamp taxes, alcohol, tobacco, vehicle, gambling and environmental duties, customs and the rest. "Other receipts" is non-tax income such as interest and public-corporation surpluses. The spending categories are simplified: they combine broad functions with selected accounting lines, and "Other & adjustments" is the residual covering general public services, most local-authority services, housing, environment, culture, depreciation and other national-accounts adjustments. The £133bn gap between receipts (~£1,235bn) and spending (~£1,368bn) is funded by borrowing, shown as an inflow so that in = out. Subsequent ONS/OBR monthly releases may revise the estimated 2025-26 outturn.

On defence. The £62bn shown is the Ministry of Defence's departmental budget for 2025-26 (2025 Spending Review: £62.2bn). On NATO's broader definition — which adds items such as armed-forces pensions and some intelligence spending — UK defence spending is around £70bn in 2025, roughly 2.4% of GDP. That wider measure, not the departmental budget, is the one behind the commitments to 2.5% by 2027 (2.6% including intelligence) and 3.5% by 2035. The chart uses the budget figure to stay consistent with the other spending lines.

Sources: OBR brief guide to the public finances and March 2026 forecast; OBR tax-by-tax/spend-by-spend explainers; ONS Public sector finances; HM Treasury Spending Review 2025; House of Commons Library.
